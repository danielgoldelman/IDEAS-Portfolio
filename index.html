Goals

Below are the three main topics I have thought about during my IDEAS Minor:

Process

The process of building and rebuilding begins with solid foundations. The IDEAS Minor has taught me to mind proper coding and engineering practices and scalability when developing a project.

Design

The design of a product is the user's first interpretation of our work. The IDEAS Minor has instilled in me the importance of reproducible, dynamic, and engaging design throughout a project.

Interactivity

The interactivity of any human-facing project is paramount to its interpretation. The IDEAS Minor has provided me industry-standard knowledge to improve user interactions with a project.

Timeline

A quick overview of how my IDEAS Minor came together.

  • Fall 2019

    How to Talk To Machines

    CS 114 provided a general overview of the CS Major, a practical understanding of how a computer works, and the ability to interact with my computer in new ways I never knew existed.

  • Spring 2020

    Intro to Design and Engineering

    IDEAS 170 allowed me to explore my natural affinity for solving complex problems in a hands-on manner, and encouraged me to interact with technologies that would ignite my passion for design.

  • Fall 2020 (1/2)

    Computer Science II

    CS 212 was the final step before declaring both the CS Major and IDEAS Minor. It was through this class that I both learned the importance of design patterns and confirmed my career in computer programming.

  • Fall 2020 (2/2)

    Sensors, Measurments, and Data Analysis

    IDEAS 173 gave me the chance to improve my skills in writing algorithms, learn new technologies that have highly practical applications, and combine software and hardware to build an interactive product.

  • Fall 2021

    Design of Programming Languages

    CS 321 opened my eyes to the mathematical reasoning behind computing, solidified my ability to break down problems programmatically, and clarified many design choices in the technologies we use every day.

  • Spring 2022

    Form and Code

    IDEAS 285 encouraged me to expand my definitions of design, intractability, and user needs, which now inform my choices when developing user-facing projects.


  • Graduation!

Picobot

Simulating cleaning bots to learn CS fundamentals.





The Picobot projects were a series of short projects to introduce students to the fundamentals of how a computer thinks. We were tasked with sweeping the whole floor with the green block in a similar fashion to how a vacuum robot would theoretically work. When reflecting on my IDEAS Minor, I believe that this project showed me the importance of patience and practical thinking in computer programming and programmatically intelligent design.

  • Class: Talking To Machines

Turing Machines

What can a computer do? What can't it do?





Computability would be a topic covered extensively in future courses, but the process of working with an actual Turing Machine this early into my programming life set the basis for understanding the inner functions of a computer when working on any low-level internal tooling. It also forced me to learn the kinds of decision making that a programmer has to make in the approach to solving problems that do not have just one solution, but many correct solutions with different efficiencies and speed.

  • Class: Talking to Machines

Put a Can On It

Figuring it out.



At the start of COVID and the break of our year, we were all left scrambling to figure out what we would do with our time and our work. Somehow, we found a way to practice developing and experiment with design goals and the physics involved in engineering by making some construct that would hold a can of beans at least 1 foot off the ground at a good ratio of weight to strength.

  • Class: Intro to Design and Engineering

Hopper

An easy project with the proper tools meets the end of the world.





This project was supposed to be the first in a series of engineering opportunities with access to a lab containing all of the materials, tools, and support we could imagine or require. Of course, that would have been too easy. Instead of a lab with power tools, we went home with masks, hand sanitizer, and a deep distrust of humanity. As you can see in the video, the original tasks of this project were accomplished, but the story of this project goes way beyond a simple exercise in obtaining a task sheet, a few materials, and some suggestions. This became a project my whole family was a part of, and the celebrations we experienced when the whole system worked exactly as designed were one of a few bright spots my family remembers from the early pandemic.

  • Class: Intro to Design and Engineering

Synthesizer

Musicing the most mathematical instrument ever developed.



Synthesizers are purely mathematical machines created with the sole purpose of creating music. Thus, mastering a synthesizer is a truly multi-disciplinary art form. You can only create a valid synthesizer with a strong knowledge of math. You can only play a synthesizer (well) with a strong knowledge of music. I wanted to prove that I could incorporate both my appreciation of math and its applications with my love of music and its ability to create emotion. Building this synthesizer gave me that opportunity.

  • Class: Intro to Design and Engineering

Drummer Boy

Pa Rum Pum Pum Pum.





This project is the true incorporation of every single aspect of my Wesleyan career. I enrolled in almost every single drumming class offered by the school, I gained strong coding skills from my CS Major, and I learned interactive design from my IDEAS Minor. This project challenged us to work with sensors to programmatically detect user input to a computer not coming through a computer keyboard. I decided to analyze the perfection to which I played an increasingly complex drumming pattern. The speed at which I played, however, also could be difficult for the computer to interpret. Thus, there were requirements to be met both by my playing of the piece and by the code I wrote to obtain, analyze, and dynamically distribute suggestions back to me. These aspects of the project forced me to consider the impact of design choices in increasingly complex codebases, which has been an instrumental understanding I carry forward into A/B testing and UX/UI.

Wesleyan's Olin Library

Dealing with scale at minimal expense to user experience.



This project challenged me to deal with staying true to the source material I wanted to model (the Olin Library windows and tiles) while also giving users the ability to influence the visuals they were presented with. This required me to focus on the robustness of the coding principles I utilized without diminishing the user experience. It was an interesting experience to know exactly what I needed to do in order to accomplish my goal of accurately representing the windows and tiles with the added requirement of a user that desired control of the final product. My solution was to allow the user to obtain influence over one portion of the windowframe (the arch), the color of the windows, and the variety in block color representing the stones in the facade.

  • Class: Form and Code

New Perspectives

Dynamic generation of new shapes from a known object.



This project was a proof of concept that I could dynamically adjust a shape's known look and create visually appealing views, sometimes emulating real life, with what could conceivably be quite random-looking. This was a study for myself as well as my viewers in whether abstract mathematically-based adjustments would make the outcomes forever unable to capture a user's attention.

  • Class: Form and Code

Code Making Art

Or a human that made code that made code that used math to make art...





My final project for this class was to build a responsive, dynamic, and programmatically secure system that could conceivably be used to quickly create variations on known objects. I used Rhino and Grasshopper, as well as various website's vectors, shapes defined in Rhino, and an English alphabet I designed to demonstrate the potential for a user to quickly use the pathway I created with the purpose of generating distinct new shapes.

  • Class: Form and Code
